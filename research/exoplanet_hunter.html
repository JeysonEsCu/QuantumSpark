Finding Transiting exoplanets in the Milky Way
Planet Hunter TESS project

The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) is a space telescope in NASA's Explorers program, designed to search for exoplanets using the transit method. This method involves analyzing a star's brightness and detecting periodic dimming caused by a planet passing in front of it. However, for this technique to work, the planetary system must be oriented so that the planet transits between the telescope and its host star. As a volunteer, I classified photometric transit data from 1,125 exoplanets, searching for new exoplanet candidates.
During this experience, I learned to detect a planet, or multiple planets, passing in front of a star and determine the orbital period by analyzing the periodicity, depth, and spacing of the dips in brightness. This allowed me to infer a planet's distance from its host star. Once I understood how a planetary transit appears in data, identifying an eclipsing binary as a result of binary systems, where two stars orbit around each other, became easier by paying attention to the shape of the curves.
