Size effect of MoO3 on the production of 99mTc

99mTc can be obtained through beta decay of 99Mo, which is produced in a fission nuclear reactor when a neutron interacts with a 98Mo nucleus, converting it into 99Mo, an instable beta emitting isotope.
The instability of 98Mo is resolved when one neutron in the nucleus undergoes beta decay, transforming into a proton and emitting an electron which is expelled (known as a beta particle). This process increases the atomic number while keeping constant the number of nucleons, transforming into an instable Technetium isotope.
Due to the continuos energy spectrum of beta particles, two important isotopes are produced, 99Tc and 99mTc with a half-life of 2.111 x 105 years and 6 hours respectively. The latter isotope is widely used in nuclear medicine and the focus of the project.
The first steps was taking the same MoO3 powder used in the radioisotope production plant (PPR) at the Peruvian Nuclear Energy Institute (IPEN) and characterize it using sieves of different sizes to create a size distribution histogram. Since this process is does not cover a wide range of particle sizes, the next step involved was wet milling with ethanol as the liquid phase, applying different milling times to achieve a more uniform size distribution.
However, mechanical methods have limitations in the particle sizes they can produce. To cover a further size range, MoO3 nanoparticles were employed. All samples were characterized using scanning electron microscopy (SEM).
Some important considerations that had to be taken into account included the neutron flux at the irradiation position in the reactor and the energy range of neutrons interacting with the molybdenum target. To determine the neutron flux in irradiation position, flux monitors were used. These consisted of thin sheets with known purity: cupper (63Cu) and indium (115In) for epithermal neutron flux and gold (197Au) for thermal neutron flux, which were irradiated together inside the irradiation can (container where the target is placed).
After, the irradiation period, the samples were returned and stored in a lead box until the radiation level was low enough to be safely handled. Once safe, the samples were measured using a gamma spectrometer to determine the neutron flux in the two energy intervals at this power. Depending on the activity of the samples, the gamma spectrometer could be with either of the two hyper pure-Ge detector geometry (two-pi or four-pi).
Before sending the samples to the reactor, they had to be prepared for placement in the irradiation can. Two methods were used: the first was making "pills" with the powders of different sizes, which were then sealed in plastic bags, labeled and separated with aluminum foil. The second method used small PET containers, where the mass of MoO3 powder was calculated by measuring the difference between the full container and the empty container.
Both techniques used to package the samples has to ensure that they remained in the same position into the container. This was achieved by using PET sponges at the top of the container to fill the empty space. After sealing the PET container, it was placed into another container named irradiation can and sealed. Before being sent to the reactor, the irradiation can had to pass a "leak test".
Once all tests were successfully completed, the irradiation container was sent to the reactor for a previously calculated time at the reactor's power the neutron flux monitor were irradiated. After the irradiation period, and a necessary decay period to reduce the radiation level, the samples were measured in a gamma spectrometer to determine the activity of the produced 99mTc. The preliminary results were reported and presented at the Iberoamerican congress of Chemsitry in 2018.
For reference on energy levels and decay schemes of the isotopes, consult the Chart of Nuclides.
